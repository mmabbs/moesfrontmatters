## 1. Context (The Setup)

**What FitIn was:**
- Ethical fitness marketplace + SaaS for solo fitness entrepreneurs ("fitpreneurs")
- Social enterprise positioning: inclusive, diverse, accessible
- 3-person team: CEO/Founder, Back-End Developer, Me

**What I was hired for:**
- UX/UI Designer + Content (micro-copy, page intros)

**What the business needed:**
- A redesigned consumer-facing site to show investors
- Affiliate acquisition strategy was primary; consumer marketing secondary
- Speed to demo-ready state for funding round

**The constraint that shaped everything:**
- COVID hit. A fitness booking platform during a pandemic. The investors liked what they saw, but the market timing killed the business.

---

## 2. The Role Expansion (What Actually Happened)

**The thesis statement:**
In a resource-constrained startup, I expanded from interface design into research leadership and brand voice development — not because I was hired for it, but because the business needed strategic thinking, not just pixels.

### Evidence Cluster 1: Research Leadership
- Designed and led full usability study (18 participants)
- Made methodology decisions that prioritized business value:
  - Recruited high-spend/high-frequency users first (a business decision, not just a sampling one)
  - Created scoring framework that accounted for incomplete builds
- Framed research goals in business terms: "drive traffic, convert users, ensure loyalty" — not "improve usability"
- Produced documentation that served dual purposes: iteration guide AND investor-facing artifact

### Evidence Cluster 2: Strategic Design Decisions

**Decision 1: Dropdown → Icon Grid**
- Problem: Backend wiring issues made dropdown unreliable; visual affordance looked like a search bar (later validated by research — users expected to type)
- Solution: Replaced with clickable icon rows linking directly to category results
- Trade-off acknowledged: Less future-proofed for new categories, but categories were broad enough that wholly new ones were unlikely. Accepted the trade-off given technical constraints and business stage.

**Decision 2: Featured Class Curation**
- Problem: Needed to communicate brand differentiation (inclusive, diverse) without explicit marketing copy
- Solution: Curated "Featured Fitness Classes" to show age diversity, format variety (in-person/online), and uncommon categories (Kung Fu for kids)
- Strategic logic: The selection *is* the message. Let the content do the positioning work.

**Decision 3: Mental Health Section Elevation**
- Problem: COVID anxiety was peaking; company values emphasized mental health alongside physical fitness
- Solution: Bumped "Featured Mental Health Classes" higher in page hierarchy
- Strategic logic: Responded to market conditions by foregrounding a differentiating category at a moment when it was most relevant

### Evidence Cluster 3: Brand Voice Work
- Wrote micro-copy throughout site (page intros, section headers, CTAs)
- Wrote a sales page (shelved — founder wasn't ready to pivot to B2C marketing, which was defensible given affiliate acquisition priority)
- Pushed for more rigorous brand voice development: tonal frameworks, vocabulary lists, Four Dimensions of Tone of Voice
- Outcome: We shipped with functional but not differentiated voice guidelines. A trade-off driven by funding timeline.

## 3. Key Findings (From the Research)

*[This section pulls from the research report — not the full findings, but the ones that demonstrate strategic thinking]*

### Findings That Went Beyond UI

**"Affiliates" as a Trust Problem**
- Finding: Users were suspicious of the word "Affiliates" — it triggered advertising/MLM associations
- Recommendation: Replace with "Service provider" or "Fitpreneur"
- Why this is strategic: Terminology shapes brand trust, not just navigation clarity

**Cultural Sensitivity as Operational Requirement**
- Finding: Users trusted the brand because of diverse class offerings, but wanted proof of genuine commitment
- Recommendation: Partner with community-endorsed training orgs; set eligibility prerequisites for "culturally sensitive" trainers
- Why this is strategic: This isn't a UI fix — it's an operational and partnerships recommendation

**Social Proof Gap**
- Finding: Users who said "no" to using the site cited FitIn being an "unknown quantity"
- Recommendation: Add brand associations, community partners, reviews
- Why this is strategic: Trust-building through third-party validation, not interface polish

## 4. What I'd Do Differently (Lessons Learned)

### Lesson 1: Front-Load Research
The usability study validated issues we could have caught earlier. Existing personas were solid but not tested against real user behavior. I now advocate for front-loading usability testing before significant design investment — even when stakeholders feel confident in existing research.

### Lesson 2: Brand Voice Deserves the Same Rigor as UI
We prioritized demo-ready visuals over voice differentiation. Defensible given funding timeline, but the brand would have been more memorable with tighter tonal guidelines. I'd push harder for vocabulary lists and voice frameworks earlier in the process.

### Lesson 3: Document for Multiple Audiences
The research report was written as an internal iteration guide but ended up being investor-facing. Knowing that earlier would have changed how I framed certain findings. Now I build documents assuming they'll travel beyond their original audience.

## 5. Outcome

**What happened:**
- Investors liked the product
- COVID killed the market
- The company shut down

**What the work demonstrated:**
- The UX foundation was strong enough to attract investor interest before official launch
- Research methodology was rigorous enough to serve as an investor-facing artifact
- Design decisions balanced technical constraints, business stage, and brand positioning

**The honest framing:**
"While FitIn.io ultimately didn't continue operations due to market conditions, the work I led — research, design, brand voice — was validated by investor interest. The failure was external, not product-related."
