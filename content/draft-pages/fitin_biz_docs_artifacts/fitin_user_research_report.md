### EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Thirteen women, four men, and one non-binary person were interviewed over Zoom on their fitness habits, preferences and practices. They were selected based on their monthly spend towards fitness and weekly workout frequency. A range was selected from a list of prospects, but the study emphasized those who spent a lot and exercised frequently. With an age range between early twenties to mid forties, these participants had various reasons to take up fitness, but it largely came down to leisure and physical health maintenance. When shopping for classes and trainers, participants considered variety, cost, proximity to home, and culture fit. Although the pandemic helped them acclimate to online classes, the majority favoured an in-person experience, with online as a fallback during lockdowns/busy schedules. Most had some experience with wellness categories, but didn't practice regularly (with exception to Yoga) or rank mental health as a bigger motivator over physical health.

Each participant completed a usability test comprised of seven questions that tests their ability to navigate and use the site's core features. Overall, 60% succeeded in completing tasks, 22% partially succeeded in completing tasks, and 17% failed. From task-to-task, success rates ranged from 6% to 100%. Partial successes ranged from 0% to 50%. Failures ranged from 0% to 44%. The site's incomplete designs, lags and bugs influenced performances.

Points of friction and participant feedback largely revolved around the site's simple visual design; limited functionality/options that affects down search efficiency and effectiveness; context to understand a page or class; and conceptual confusion with the demo, booking process, directory and affiliates. Participants admired the site for its unobtrusive visual design and trusted the brand because of its focus on offering diverse and inclusive classes. That said, Fitln would have to extend its values into action by ensuring Fitpreneurs are culturally sensitive, or have undertaken cultural sensitivity training.

### GOALS

1. Understand how the brand's target users relate to fitness
    
    Knowing the fitness habits and preferences of target users would lead to smart brand marketing that is relevant, persuasive and effective in driving traffic to the site, converting new users and ensuring current members remain loyal.
    
2. Run a usability test on users to discover points of friction
    
    Testing usability, functional integrity and visual design would reveal errors, and conceptual and technical bottlenecks that impede conversions and impact brand trust.

### METHODOLOGY

This usability study adopted a qualitative and quantitative approach to learn about FitIn's demographic as people, as much as app users. It tells us the why, in addition to the what and how. Such an approach encourages softly guided conversations that uncover the reasoning and motivations behind beliefs and behaviour that guide user experiences in the world of fitness and on FitIn's website.

1. RECRUIT PARTICIPANTS
    
    55 prospects answered a Facebook Ad to complete a survey asking them for their monthly spend on fitness and the number of classes they attend per week. 18 participants -12 women, 5 men, 1 non-binary were selected to cover a range of frequency and spend. High frequency and spend was prioritized.
    
2. CONDUCT INTERVIEWS
    
    Over Zoom, participants answered questions about their preferred fitness types, criteria for classes and trainers, meaning of wellness, experience with apps and in-person/online exercise. Some shared stories that revealed fitness not just as a means to an end, but a significant part of their identity.
    
3. RUN USABILITY TESTS
    
    Over Zoom, participants completed tasks that involved finding and booking classes based on categories and businesses, using the site's core features. They also completed self-assessments on their performance of certain multi-step tasks in order for Fitin to gauge their satisfaction with the experience.
    
4. ANALYZE DATA
    
    The major takeaways from the interviews were summarized in one sheet per participant, with information that would help Fitln with its business, UX and marketing goals. Test results were collated into tables and formulas to find issues with usability, utility, functional integrity, and visual design.
    

#### Defining "Success"

Scoring

Successful tasks received a score of 1. Unsuccessful tasks received 0. Given the iteration's incomplete design and buggy code, this study showed leniency in task completion errors/delays related to "avoidable" design/coding issues (time and budget permitting). It was equally important to observe/inquire about alternative approaches to completing a task in order to learn more about the participants themselves. For these two reasons, the study attributed a 0.5 score to delayed completions - but only particular kinds. A task received 0.5 if the participant successfully completed it in a second attempt after the first search erroneously returned nothing due to a bug. A task also received 0.5 if the participant found a listing despite only using one of requested filters (a common habit). A task did not receive 0.5 if the participant stumbled upon the solution while blind-navigating/clicking arbitrarily in hopes of finding it, whether or not the discovery took long. Deeply nested navigations to pages that could hold the solution weren't counted based on usability rules (noticeable, easy to use, etc.)

Evidence and percentages

The study uses quotes, observations and percentages of shared/overlapping feedback as evidence of its findings. Although some feedback held a majority exceeding 50% most did not. Nonetheless, the wide variety of reactions and suggestions pointed to a shared underlying need/concern. In such cases, the percentages are cumulative. For instance, if only four participants wanted a business filter and only two wished for a subcategory filter, the study considered them as a unit "wanting more filters", making up 33% of the total (6 shared/18 overall).

#### Limitations

- Qualitative data and interpretation
    
    People don't always say exactly what they mean or feel, in a way that is crystal clear. This is particularly true in a formal context that explores a potentially sensitive subject like health and wellness, or value judgments in a self-assessment. Furthermore, most people surf the web without much forethought or reflection, and thus don't always have the capacity to describe their experience or pinpoint the "why" behind their feelings (especially with visual design). Furthermore, while industry benchmarks are valuable as frames of reference, "fast" and "slow" is relative to the individual. They don't account for uncontrollable variables, like old age and internet speeds, that influence a person's good or bad experience.
    
- Self-Assessment Scale only includes 13 participants and certain tasks
    
    The scale was introduced only when participants began to challenge observations about their performance and level of satisfaction. For the sake of time, the study included only four tasks, which were either straightforward or multi-step.
    
- Tech issues with Zoom and internet connectivity
    
    Some participants had difficulty logging into Zoom and/or using some of its functions (e.g. screen-sharing without switching off speaker video). Weak internet connections often led to lag and/or dropped calls.
    
- Bugs, incomplete designs, limited offerings
    
    The current iteration has bugs, lag, and bland placeholder design elements that confused participants, distorted performance strength, and offered common sensical design feedback. Limited class offerings sometimes often led either to false assumptions about availability, or quick finds on the results page, such that filters weren't needed.
    
- User Testing tools
    
    Due to budget constraints, heatmaps, A/B testing, Card Sorts, and Tree Tests, were not used in the test. Due to connectivity issues, task speeds were not recorded.

### FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

#### VISUAL DESIGN

- Finding: the design is plain/bland
    
    At best, the UI is neither offensive nor confusing. It's persuasive in terms of feeling "safe". Several commented on the "old user"-friendliness simplicity of the design. Storefront banners are too big relative to copy and business info.
    
- **Evidence:**
    
    - 33% stated or implied the design as plain/bland.
        
    - Quotes: "Feels like a typical fitness site.", "It feels too plain", "It needs to be more visually interesting".
        
- **RECOMMENDATIONS:**
    
    - Revert to original prototype designs (where applicable).
        
    - Center page layout for wide viewport sizes to reduce white space.
        
    - Add more depth and texture to emphasize visual hierarchy.
        
    - Set guides/reqs for storefront branding and class listing photos.
        
    - Replace blue banner with a better placeholder image at a reduced size.
        
    - Improve visual hierarchy.
        

#### BOOKING PROCESS

- Finding 1: the "register/login" process is confusing
    
    Most participants reflexively inputted their number without realizing they needed to register first. Many failed to notice the 'Register Now?' button. The button copy as a question sounds like a sequential step after the input.
    
- **Evidence:**
    
    - 50% either inputted phone number or assumed they had to.
        
    - 39% failed to acknowledge 'Register Now?' button.
        
    - Quote: "If it had prompted me to input my email, I would've instinctively known to register first."
        
- **RECOMMENDATIONS:**
    
    - Change to "Sign Up" (implies less effort).
        
    - Split "Login/Sign Up" into two navigation pathways (with two buttons).
        
- Finding 2: Listing Fitin in Directory is counterintuitive
    
    Most of those who knew to go to the Directory had spotted Fitln when finding Kelly Kinetix. Otherwise, they checked other pages before giving up.
    
- **Evidence:**
    
    - 56% assumed the demo CTA was located elsewhere (Sitemap, FAQ, etc.).
        
    - Quote: "After trying all those places, I would've given up and just googled it or reach out by email / social."
        
- **RECOMMENDATIONS:**
    
    - Add demo CTA above the fold on homepage and in FAQ.
        

#### FILTERS

- Finding 1: The drop down menu isn't noticeable
    
    Many participants either failed to notice the filters bar completely or only noticed it much later.
    
- **Evidence:**
    
    - 78% felt the filters were inadequate.
        
    - 33% failed to notice the filters immediately.
        
- Finding 2: The limited range, parameters and taxonomy impact efficiency
    
    Many commented on the desire to change and add filters. There were bottlenecks due to confusion ("format") and a search for unavailable parameters ("category").
    
- **Evidence:**
    
    - 56% struggled with one or several of the reasons above.
        
    - Quote: "I would prefer 'low to high' because a wide budget would require checking off all these boxes".
        
- **RECOMMENDATIONS:**
    
    - Change the header and caret colour to a noticeable colour.
        
    - Move the caret closer to the header.
        
    - Collapse the menu by default.
        
    - Add "Clear All" function.
        
    - Add "Low to High", Category filters.
        
    - Consider 'Business Types' filter.
        
    - Show all future availability in calendar.
        
    - Drop 'Format', default to 'In-Person'.
        

#### SEARCH BAR

- Finding 1: A search bar without typing is counterintuitive
    
    Nearly all participants expected to type, partly because of the icon and habit.
    
- **Evidence:**
    
    - 67% expected the ability to type.
        
    - 28% used bar to look up free PT session.
        
    - Quote: "I prefer to type over using filters."
        
- **RECOMMENDATIONS:**
    
    - Enable typing.
        
    - Remove magnifying glass icon until typing function is implemented.
        
    - Lower position of search bar.
        
    - Consider incorporating business types.
        

#### RESULTS PAGE

- Finding: It needs more clarity, convenience and consistency
    
    Participants expected the ability to sort results by distance, time, price, etc. Listings needed more context. The results should have a header stating the searched category. Several expected a map and/or a "Find a Class Near You" function.
    
- **Evidence:**
    
    - 22% reflected the sentiments above.
        
    - Quotes: "I thought this Yoga class was an event based on the photo.", "I'd rather not go day-to-day to find an available class."
        
- **RECOMMENDATIONS:**
    
    - Implement map.
        
    - Add category header on results load.
        
    - Add postal code lookup.
        
    - Add sorting based on date/times, distance, format, price.
        
    - Change calendar to show all future availability.
        
    - Add more description to listings.
        
    - Use accurate/relevant photos.
        

#### DIRECTORY

- Finding: The page is unclear and unusable
    
    Participants largely guessed businesses would be under "Directory". They didn't have enough context to understand the page. Some wanted a map and sorting feature.
    
- **Evidence:**
    
    - 72% shared the sentiments above.
        
    - 50% didn't register list as businesses off their names alone.
        
    - Quote: "When I hear the word 'Affiliates', my guard immediately goes up. I wonder if their values line up with mine…"
        
- **RECOMMENDATIONS:**
    
    - Include lead-in copy under header explaining the directory.
        
    - Add business profile pics and other context to listings.
        
    - Add Sort function based on business type, distance, etc.
        
    - Consider replacing "Affiliates" with "Service provider / Fitpreneur".
        

#### BRAND TRUST

- Finding: Fitin is trustworthy - but it needs to prove itself
    
    Participants felt the site conveyed a friendly and approachable brand. The element that conveyed most trust was the diversity of classes. Those who said "no" to using the site mentioned FitIn's current status as an "unknown quantity". They want to see proof of FitIn's genuine commitment to social good, like cultural sensitivity training for trainers.
    
- **RECOMMENDATIONS:**
    
    - Add social proof, like brand's associations, community partners, etc.
        
    - Partner with community-endorsed training orgs.
        
    - Increase transparency where applicable.
        
    - Set eligibility/prerequisites for trainers to qualify as "culturally sensitive".
        
    - Exercise due diligence on all trainers/orgs.

### BUGS & ISSUES

- **DESIGN DIFFERENCES:** Designs in the prototypes were not fully adopted into the live website.
    
- **SEARCH SPEED:** Asset loading and dynamic changes based on user input are still lagging.
    
- **SEARCH BAR:**
    
    - The category order in the bar on the homepage is not the same as on the search results page.
        
    - The scroll indicator is not visible until prompted by scroll action.
        
- **LOAD INDICATOR:** There is no prominent graphical feedback to inform users a page is loading.
    
- **2FA LOADING AND STATUS:** The loading bar is indefinitely frozen upon "logging in".

    1. Narrow the viewport size to centre the page layout.

    1. Add intro copy on Fitin's partners, business types.
        
    2. Alphabetize list.

    4. Explore alternatives to "Directory" and "Affiliates".

    2. Add micro copy in Sign Up modal reaffirming commitment to privacy.
        
- **SEARCH BAR**
    
    1. Swap magnifying glass icon with caret to indicate dropdown menu.
        
    2. Make category order consistent on homepage and results page.
